Importance of Population

Many of you may have heard of the R factor in the context of the coronavirus pandemic that plagues us. This comes from epidemiology and is known as the effective reproductive number. Each person in the population can be modelled as a susceptible and non-susceptible host (immune). If R>1, the number of cases will increase, such as at the start of an epidemic. Where R=1, the disease is endemic, and where R<1 there will be a decline in the number of cases. R is the exponent of an exponential curve that can determine how quickly the epidemic spreads. It goes without saying that countries with larger populations would be affected to a greater extent. However, there are other factors at play. Countries with greater incoming traffic of people (or hosts) either being a popular holiday destination or openess of financial markets making it a business hub could greatly impact the R factor. This lends itself to varying levels of R across countries and most likely will cause clustering in certain continents as we see below.

Does Lockdown Really Matter

To illustrate this point let's make it fair. Let's calculate the logarithm of deaths per capita. This will take into account population. You can hover over the lines below in order to reveal the country (coloured by their continent, for example European countries are blue). We have some of the highest deaths per capita countries below. You would expect that countries that enforced an earlier lockdown would have been able to reduce their R factor. However, this is simply not true. Despite the United Kingdom being more populated than Italy and enforcing a later lockdown their progression is very similar. Sweden with no lockdown faired better than most of the other European countries. This makes you question how effective a lockdown really is and whether getting the timing incorrect can actually reduce herd immunity and incur more deaths than without a lockdown.
